Rome Wine & Aperitivo Culture: The Complete 2026 Guide

Published: 19 April 2026 14 min read Category: Food & Dining Part of: Rome Food Culture Guide

Rome's relationship with wine is ancient, practical, and often misunderstood by visitors. The city that supplies the world's most famous wine regions (Barolo, Brunello, Amarone) with its tourism and marketing energy actually drinks its own local Castelli Romani whites at home, pours house carafe at every traditional trattoria, and approaches wine as a daily food rather than a performance. Understanding this shifts everything about how you drink in Rome.

This guide covers the Castelli Romani wine region, the aperitivo tradition, the natural wine scene, the best wine bars by neighborhood, and the practical knowledge you need to drink well in Rome without overpaying or undervaluing what's available.

The Castelli Romani Wine Region: Rome's Local Wine

The Castelli Romani is a volcanic plateau approximately 25 kilometers southeast of Rome, centered on the Alban Hills. The volcanic soil (tufo and pozzolana) gives the wines their characteristic minerality. The elevation provides cool nights that preserve acidity even in hot summers. The proximity to Rome means these have been the city's table wines since antiquity -- Cicero, Cato, and Pliny all mention the wines of the Alban Hills.

Frascati DOC

Grape: Primarily Malvasia di Candia and Trebbiano Toscano, with Greco and Bombino allowed.

Character: Crisp, dry, with mineral and citrus notes. Light-bodied. Best drunk young. Pale golden color.

In Rome: The default house white at traditional trattorias. A carafe will cost EUR 6-10. Frascati Superiore DOCG is a more serious, aged version worth seeking on wine lists.

At the source: The town of Frascati is 35 minutes from Rome by train (Termini to Frascati, EUR 3.50 single). Several cantinas offer wine tasting and direct purchase at winery prices.

Marino DOC

Character: Fuller-bodied than Frascati, more aromatic. Often considered the best of the Castelli Romani whites by serious wine drinkers but less marketed.

Why it matters: Marino is frequently the actual wine poured as "house white" at Roman restaurants, regardless of the label on the carafe. This is not fraud -- it reflects Marino's general superiority for the role. A good Marino is one of Italy's most underrated wines.

Cesanese del Piglio DOCG

Grape: Cesanese, a Lazio-native red grape variety found almost nowhere else.

Character: Medium-bodied, cherry and violet aromas, earthy with good acidity. Pairs excellently with Roman meat dishes (lamb, oxtail, braised beef).

Why it's interesting: Cesanese is one of Italy's most characterful native grape varieties and is undervalued precisely because it grows only in Lazio. If you drink wine at all, try Cesanese del Piglio at least once while in Rome.

Rome's Aperitivo Culture

The Roman aperitivo is a distinct tradition from Milan's. Where Milanese aperitivo often includes an extensive food spread that functions as a pre-dinner meal, Roman aperitivo is lighter and more ritualistic -- a drink and a small snack (olive, chip, or small bruschetta) that marks the transition between afternoon and evening. It takes place between approximately 6pm and 9pm.

The classic Roman aperitivo drinks are the Campari soda (Campari mixed with soda water, served with an orange slice -- bitter, refreshing, and genuinely Roman), the Aperol spritz (Aperol, prosecco, and soda water -- sweeter and more internationally fashionable), and a glass of the house white wine.

Romans take aperitivo seriously as a social ritual. It is when the day's conversations happen, when plans for the evening are made, and when the neighborhood bar functions as a community commons. For expats, participating in the local bar's aperitivo hour is one of the most effective ways to integrate into neighborhood life. This is not a tourist experience -- it is how Romans actually live.

Marco Esposito, sommelier and wine bar owner, Trastevere — 14 years in Roman wine

"When I opened my bar the question was whether to do a Milan-style aperitivo with food spreads or the Roman way. I chose Roman: one drink, small snack, good conversation. The international customers want the Milan version because it's Instagram-friendly. But my locals want to talk. They don't want to fill a plate. Aperitivo is not dinner in Rome. It is transition. The best time of day for conversation."
"The natural wine scene in Rome has matured significantly in the last five years. We now have bars that serve serious natural wines -- biodynamic, minimal intervention -- alongside bars that use 'natural' as a marketing word for bad wine. The distinction matters. Look for sommeliers who can explain the producer, the grape, and the vintage. If they can't, the wine is probably not interesting."

The Natural Wine Scene in Rome

Rome has developed one of Italy's strongest natural wine scenes over the past decade. Several bars and wine shops -- particularly in Trastevere, Testaccio, Pigneto, and Ostiense -- have built serious programs around minimal-intervention wines from Italian and European producers. The best of these places have educated staff, interesting producers, and the willingness to talk about what they're pouring.

The distinction between serious natural wine and wine labeled natural for marketing purposes is real and worth understanding. Serious natural wine has lower sulfites, often volatile aromas (oxidative notes in whites; barnyard, funky characteristics in reds that divide opinion), and a transparency about producer and vintage. If a bar can't tell you the producer and vintage of their "natural" wine, it is probably not interesting.

The house carafe at dinner: Do not automatically order a bottle at a Roman trattoria. Ask for the vino della casa (house wine) or vino sfuso (loose wine by the carafe). It will come as a quarter, half, or full liter. It will typically be a Castelli Romani white or a simple Montepulciano. It will cost EUR 6-12 for half a liter and will be perfectly appropriate for the food. This is how Romans drink at dinner.

Wine Touring from Rome: The Castelli Romani Day Trip

The Castelli Romani wine region is one of Rome's best day trips for wine-interested expats. The train from Termini to Frascati takes 35 minutes and costs EUR 3.50. The town itself has several cantinas open for tasting and purchase. A full circuit of the Castelli Romani hills -- Frascati, Grottaferrata, Marino, Castelgandolfo (the Pope's summer residence) -- is achievable by train and bus in a day and gives context to the wines you've been drinking all week.

The best season for the wine tour is October, during the wine harvest (vendemmia). Many cantinas run harvest days where visitors can participate in picking and pressing. The Marino Sagra dell'Uva (grape festival, usually first Sunday of October) is one of Lazio's great wine events.
