Rome Street Food & Bakeries: The 2026 Guide

Published: 19 April 2026 14 min read Category: Food & Dining Part of: Rome Food Culture Guide

Rome's street food is one of the city's best-kept secrets from first-time visitors. While tourist discussions focus on restaurant reservations and set menus, the food that Romans actually eat while moving through their city is extraordinary, cheap, and accessible on every block. A supplì from the right forno is better than a restaurant appetizer anywhere in Rome. A maritozzo from the right bar is better than most pastry in Europe. Pizza al taglio from a neighborhood forno is one of the world's great casual foods.

This guide covers each of Rome's canonical street foods, where to find the best versions by neighborhood, and the bakery culture that forms the daily food rhythm of Roman life.

Supplì al Telefono: The Essential Roman Street Food

Supplì al telefono is Rome's definitive street food. The name refers to the telephone wire formed when the mozzarella inside is pulled apart -- a string of melted cheese stretching between the two halves. The construction is specific: tomato risotto (rice cooked in tomato sauce, sometimes with meat ragù) is shaped into an oval, stuffed with a piece of fresh mozzarella, coated in breadcrumbs, and deep-fried to order.

The quality range is extreme. A good supplì has a crispy, even breadcrumb exterior that is deeply golden rather than pale; an interior where the rice is al dente and the tomato flavor is genuine; and a center of melted mozzarella that forms the telephone wire on demand. A bad supplì has pale, uneven coating; mushy or overcooked rice; and a mozzarella filling that is either absent or has been substituted with processed cheese.

Where to find the best in Rome:

Mordi e Vai (Testaccio market, stall 15) consistently makes one of Rome's best, using a proper tomato ragù. Supplì Roma (Via San Francesco a Ripa 137, Trastevere) is a dedicated supplì specialist with both classic and creative variations -- the truffle version is controversial but good. Forno Campo de' Fiori (Campo de' Fiori 22) makes a reliable traditional version at market prices. In the Testaccio market itself, at least two stalls compete seriously for best supplì in the building.

Price: EUR 1.50-3.00 each at genuine forno or market. EUR 3.50-5.00 at tourist-oriented food shops.

Pizza al Taglio: Roman Pizza by the Cut

Roman pizza al taglio (pizza by the cut) is a different food from Neapolitan pizza. The dough is Roman-style: long-fermented (often 48-72 hours), high-hydration, resulting in a base that is crispy on the bottom, chewy in the middle, and airy throughout. It is baked in rectangular trays, displayed at room temperature or kept warm, and sold by weight (EUR per 100 grams) at a counter where you indicate the section and amount you want.

The best Roman pizza al taglio is an exercise in restraint. The potato and rosemary (patate e rosmarino) is perhaps the most characteristic -- thin-sliced potatoes, fresh rosemary, excellent olive oil, sea salt, white base. The courgette flower and anchovy (fiori di zucca e alici) is the spring seasonal standard. The margherita with buffalo mozzarella should demonstrate the quality of the tomato sauce first and everything else second.

Best by neighborhood:

Forno Campo de' Fiori (Campo de' Fiori 22) is the historic standard but crowds make it difficult. Forno Roscioli (Via dei Chiavari 34) is technically superior and has a longer queue. In Prati, the fornos on Via Candia and Via Vespasiano make excellent neighborhood versions. In Testaccio, Panificio Bonci at Mercato Testaccio is worth the reputation. In San Lorenzo, the forni around Via dei Volsci operate at honest working-neighborhood prices with genuine quality.

Price: EUR 1.80-3.50 per 100g at genuine forno; a satisfying lunch portion (two pieces, ~200-250g) costs EUR 4-8.

Maritozzo: The Roman Sweet Bun

The maritozzo is Rome's contribution to European pastry culture: a soft, slightly sweet yeast roll filled with an extravagant amount of freshly whipped cream. It has existed in Roman food culture since medieval times -- the marito (husband) etymology suggests a tradition of men gifting them to their intended on the first Friday of Lent, with a ring or gold coin hidden inside.

The contemporary maritozzo is straightforwardly excellent: a brioche-adjacent dough that is softer and less buttery than French brioche, split and filled with cream that is lightly sweetened and genuinely fresh. The ratio should be generous -- more cream than bun. A mediocre maritozzo is cream-sparse and dough-heavy. An excellent one leaves cream on your face and hands.

The maritozzo had its international moment circa 2020 when food media discovered it. This made it briefly trendy and permanently available across Rome at varying quality levels. The tourist-area versions are generally serviceable; the neighborhood bar versions made fresh daily are genuinely superior.

Where to find the best: Forno del Ghetto (Via del Portico d'Ottavia 2, Jewish Ghetto) -- the historic reference. Roscioli Caffè (Piazza Benedetto Cairoli 16) -- the upscale version with excellent cream quality. In your local neighborhood bar (especially in Prati, Flaminio, and Salario), fresh maritozzi made that morning are often available and are the best possible version for the price.

Price: EUR 2.00-4.50 depending on neighborhood and establishment.

The Roman Bakery (Forno) Culture

The forno (bakery) is central to Roman neighborhood life in a way that has largely disappeared from Anglo-American cities. Every functioning Roman neighborhood has at least one forno that opens between 6:30 and 7:30am and serves fresh bread, cornetti, pastries, and pizza al taglio to the neighborhood population before work. This is not artisanal bread tourism -- it is daily infrastructure.

The bread culture is distinct from French or German traditions. Roman bread (pane di Lariano, the local sourdough made with Lariano wheat and olive oil, is the most distinctive regional variety) is denser, has a harder crust, and is designed to last without refrigeration for two days. It is excellent for soaking up braised meat juices and olive oil. It is not designed for delicate sandwiches.
