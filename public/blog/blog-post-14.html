Rome Dining Etiquette: The Honest Guide for Expats

Published: 19 April 2026 12 min read Category: Food & Dining Part of: Rome Food Culture Guide

Eating in Rome has conventions that aren't immediately obvious, and getting them wrong is a reliable way to both pay more and enjoy less. This isn't about rigid rules or judgmental waiters -- it's about understanding a food culture that operates differently from what most expats bring as default assumptions. Once you understand the logic, every meal becomes easier and more pleasurable.

The Course Structure

A full Italian meal has four courses: antipasto (starter), primo (first course -- pasta, risotto, or soup), secondo (main course -- meat, fish, or egg dish, served with a separately ordered side contorno), and dolce (dessert). In practice, Romans rarely eat all four courses at every meal.

At lunch, one or two courses is normal: a pasta (primo) only, or a pasta and a secondo. At dinner, two to three courses is standard for a proper meal. Only at Sunday family lunches or formal occasions does the full four-course structure occur routinely.

The important convention for expats: ordering only a secondo (main course) without a primo is unusual and may result in raised eyebrows from waitstaff, particularly at lunch. If you want only a main course, a salad as a "starter" satisfies the expectation of multiple courses.

The Coperto (Cover Charge)

The coperto is a per-person charge (typically EUR 1.50-4.00) that appears on your bill separately from the food prices. It covers the bread, the tablecloth service, and the cost of occupying a table for a meal duration. It is standard, legitimate, and non-negotiable at most restaurants. It is not a tip.

Complaining about the coperto is considered rude and marks you as a tourist who doesn't understand Italian restaurant economics. If a restaurant has a coperto listed on the menu (which it is legally required to do), you agreed to pay it when you sat down. Budget EUR 2-3 per person per meal as the coperto component of your dining costs.

Tipping

Tipping is not mandatory in Italy. Service is built into food prices. When you enjoy a meal and wish to acknowledge good service, leaving EUR 2-5 on the table is appropriate and appreciated. The percentage-based tipping system (15-20% as in the US) does not apply and is not expected. Some tourist-area restaurants may include a "servizio" (service charge) on the bill; check before leaving additional cash.

The Coffee Rules

Cappuccino is a breakfast drink in Italy. Ordering one after 11am identifies you as a tourist. Italians will not refuse to serve it to you -- they are polite -- but it is genuinely considered odd. An espresso or caffè macchiato is appropriate at any hour. After dinner, the correct choice is an espresso (caffè), a digestivo (grappa, amaro, or limoncello), or nothing. A cappuccino after dinner is considered bizarre by Italian standards.

Common Expat Mistakes

DO

  • Order the daily specials (seasonal, fresh, what the kitchen is confident in)
  • Ask for the house wine carafe
  • Stand at the counter for coffee (faster, cheaper)
  • Make eye contact with the waiter to get service
  • Ask "Cosa consiglia?" (What do you recommend?)
  • Allow meals to take time -- this is not a transaction
  • Order a digestivo after dinner at traditional restaurants

DON'T

  • Ask for cream in carbonara
  • Order cappuccino after 11am
  • Ask for Parmesan on seafood pasta
  • Sit at a table in a bar for coffee (you'll pay table service premium)
  • Complain about the coperto
  • Expect your waiter to check on you constantly
  • Ask for the bill before you're ready -- it won't come unless requested

"Il conto, per favore" -- Asking for the Bill

In Italy, the bill does not come automatically. The cultural norm is that you are welcome to sit and talk as long as you wish after finishing eating. To request the bill, say "Il conto, per favore" or make a writing gesture toward the waiter. This is not rude -- it is the correct protocol. The waiter is not ignoring you; they are respecting your time at the table.

The Table Water Convention

Still water (acqua naturale) and sparkling water (acqua frizzante) are charged separately at restaurants. A bottle costs EUR 2-4. Asking for tap water (acqua del rubinetto) is technically acceptable but is met with varying degrees of hospitality depending on the establishment. In Rome, the tap water is safe, clean, and genuinely good (Rome's water is famous for its quality); the restaurant water charge is a revenue line. Decide your preference before arriving.

When Service Seems Slow

Italian restaurant service operates on a different timeline than Northern European or American service culture. You will not be checked on frequently. Your needs are your responsibility to communicate. Catching the waiter's eye, a direct but polite "Scusi" (excuse me), or a slight raise of the hand is how you get service. Snapping fingers or shouting is rude everywhere; in Italy it will be met with a long wait.

Navigating the Menu

The best practice: read the menu carefully, ask about the daily specials (piatti del giorno or speciali di oggi), and lean toward whatever the kitchen describes with most enthusiasm. At a traditional Roman trattoria, the daily specials are the dishes made freshest that day with market ingredients. The fixed menu items are reliable standards; the specials are the kitchen's current best work.

If you have dietary restrictions, communicate them clearly before ordering ("Sono vegetariano/a" -- I'm vegetarian; "Sono allergico/a a..." -- I'm allergic to...). Italian restaurant culture is accommodating of clear, specific requests; it is less accommodating of complex modifications that restructure dishes.
