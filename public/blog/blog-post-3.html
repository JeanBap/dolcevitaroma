Living in Trastevere & Testaccio: Rome's Expat Heartland

Published: 19 April 2026 15 min read Category: Neighborhoods Part of: Rome Neighborhoods Guide

If you ask any expat in Rome where you should live, the first word out of most mouths is Trastevere. After a few years, many of them migrate to Testaccio. Both of these neighborhoods are the right answer, but for different stages of expat life. This guide explains what each actually delivers, who thrives in each, and why the EUR 200-400 monthly rent difference between them matters less than you'd think.

Trastevere at a Glance

1BR rent: EUR 1,100 - 1,700/mo

Character: Bohemian, social, internationalized

Best for: 25-40s, social expats, remote workers

Expat community: Very high density

Noise level: High on weekends

Metro access: None direct; Trastevere train station

Testaccio at a Glance

1BR rent: EUR 950 - 1,400/mo

Character: Authentic Roman, food-focused, calmer

Best for: Food lovers, families, settled expats

Expat community: High density, maturing

Noise level: Moderate

Metro access: Piramide (Line B), 8-min walk

Trastevere: The Neighborhood That Earned Its Reputation

Trastevere's reputation as Rome's most liveable expat neighborhood is not marketing. It reflects a genuine accumulation of social infrastructure built over decades of international residents choosing to put down roots here. The result is a neighborhood where you can be lonely for approximately your first two weeks before the community absorbs you.

The physical character of Trastevere is extraordinary: narrow lanes of warm-toned brick and plaster, window boxes of bougainvillea and jasmine, piazzas sized for human community rather than traffic, and a geographical separation from the rest of Rome (it sits west of the Tiber, technically in Ripa municipality) that creates a genuine sense of neighborhood identity. Residents say "I live in Trastevere" with a particular pride that residents of most other Roman neighborhoods don't feel.

The social infrastructure breaks down into several overlapping networks. The DVR community meetups consistently draw the highest turnout from Trastevere residents. There are multiple English-language book exchanges, a language tandem network, and a series of international events run from venues that have been doing it for long enough to know how to do it well. The Piazza di Santa Maria in Trastevere functions as the neighborhood living room -- on any warm evening you can sit on the fountain steps and meet someone.

The Gentrification Question

Every article about Trastevere is obligated to mention gentrification, and this one is no exception. The neighborhood has changed. Rents that were EUR 700-800 for a one-bedroom twelve years ago are EUR 1,100-1,400 today. Several authentic trattorias have been replaced by tourist-serving restaurants with QR code menus and non-Italian ownership. The Via della Lungaretta has become more bar-heavy and less family-residential.

The counter-argument, made by long-term residents, is that Trastevere's gentrification has been accompanied by a genuine improvement in many services -- better cafes, more interesting wine bars, cleaner streets -- and that the working-class Roman community, while smaller, has not entirely departed. The alimentari run by the same families for thirty years still exist. The elderly residents still argue in the street. The neighborhood's soul is under commercial pressure but not yet dead.

What this means practically: Trastevere is still worth the premium for first-year expats building their community and social life. After two to three years, many residents find that the social infrastructure they built is portable, and moving to quieter Testaccio (or even Pigneto) takes the community with them while reducing monthly costs by EUR 200-400.

Best Streets in Trastevere

Via della Lungaretta is Trastevere's main artery and the center of its social life. It runs east-west across the neighborhood and is genuinely lively at all hours. The best apartments here are on upper floors away from street-level noise. The concentration of wine bars and restaurants makes it simultaneously the most convenient and most noisy location in the neighborhood.

Vicolo dei Renzi and the lanes immediately off it are the most photogenic and, when you're lucky enough to find an apartment here, the most special residential streets in the neighborhood. Narrow, car-free, with minimal tourist throughput and maximum Roman character. Apartments here rarely come to market and command a premium when they do.

Streets near Santa Cecilia church (Via di S. Cecilia, Via dei Genovesi) offer the calmer residential eastern fringe of Trastevere with a quieter character while remaining within five minutes' walk of the Via della Lungaretta scene. Several experienced expats specifically seek this zone.

Testaccio: Where Expats Graduate To

Testaccio was Rome's meat-packing district for over a century -- the massive slaughterhouse (Mattatoio) closed in the 1970s and has since been converted into a cultural space and market. The neighborhood's working-class roots are visible in its grid-plan streets, 1890s tenement architecture, and a food culture built around the offal-heavy Roman culinary tradition (quinto quarto -- the fifth quarter of the animal -- originated here as workers' food).

Today, Testaccio is Rome's most food-focused neighborhood. The Mercato di Testaccio is one of the city's best markets. Via Galvani is lined with trattorias that have been serving the same Roman dishes for decades. The street food scene -- particularly the supplì stands and the tripe sandwiches at Mordi e Vai -- is as good as Rome gets.

The expat population is smaller than Trastevere's but more settled. Average tenancy is longer; people come to Testaccio when they know Rome well enough to value authenticity over social density. The DVR community presence is strong and several of our most active members live here.

James Holbrook, 42, IT Consultant (UK) — Trastevere, 3 years; now Testaccio

"I stayed in Trastevere for three years and I don't regret a day of it. The community I built there was the foundation of my Rome life. Then I moved to Testaccio and my quality of life went up. Lower rent, better market, quieter streets, same distance from the city center. What surprised me was that I didn't lose the community. The DVR network follows you. You just choose differently what to put at the center of your daily life."

Transport from Trastevere and Testaccio

Neither neighborhood has a metro stop within comfortable walking distance, which is a genuine consideration for regular commuters. Trastevere has a train station (Stazione Trastevere) that connects to Ostiense and beyond. Bus lines 8, 23, and H run frequently through both neighborhoods. The tram 3 connects Testaccio to Colosseo, Villa Borghese, and further east.

Both neighborhoods are bikeable -- the terrain is flat and the streets, while narrow, have relatively low car traffic inside the residential areas. Several experienced expats cycle to the center (Piazza Venezia in 15-20 minutes from Trastevere) rather than taking public transport.

Late night: bus lines run but with reduced frequency after midnight. The Uber presence in Rome has improved significantly and is reliable from both neighborhoods. Being without a car is entirely feasible here.

The Food Scene: Eating Well in Both Neighborhoods

Trastevere's food scene has both improved and diluted over the past decade. The excellent natural wine bars (several with serious wine programs and thoughtful food menus) have emerged alongside mediocre tourist restaurants. The signal for quality is the same as everywhere: Romans at the tables, handwritten menus, no photos of food.

Testaccio's food scene is more consistently excellent because it has remained more authentically local. The market is the centrepiece, but the trattorias on Via Galvani and Via Giovanni Branca have a tradition of proper Roman cooking that predates the expat presence and will outlast it. Roscioli (the salumeria and wine bar on Via dei Giubbonari, technically just outside Testaccio) is one of Rome's genuine food institutions and close enough for regular visits.
