Budget Neighborhoods in Rome for Expats: Esquilino, Ostiense & San Lorenzo

Published: 19 April 2026 14 min read Category: Neighborhoods Part of: Rome Neighborhoods Guide

The idea that you need to pay EUR 1,400 a month to live in real Rome is a myth perpetuated by people who haven't looked beyond Trastevere. Three neighborhoods -- Esquilino, Ostiense, and San Lorenzo -- offer central, well-connected, interesting expat life in Rome for EUR 700-1,000 a month in rent. Each is different in character, and each attracts a distinct type of expat. All three are genuinely good places to live, not merely affordable ones.

Esquilino: Rome's Most Connected, Most Multicultural Neighborhood

Esquilino sits immediately east of Termini station, Rome's central rail hub. This gives it the best public transport access in the city -- every metro line, most bus routes, and all intercity trains converge here. The practical consequence for daily life is that you can reach any part of Rome within 20-25 minutes.

Esquilino is Rome's most multicultural neighborhood. The areas around Piazza Vittorio Emanuele II and Via Principe Amedeo have a dense presence of Chinese, Bangladeshi, and Ethiopian-run businesses alongside traditional Roman shops. The covered market at Piazza Vittorio (open Monday-Saturday, 7am-3pm) is arguably the most interesting shopping in Rome: fresh turmeric, exceptional Asian spices, fresh fish at competitive prices, and Italian produce vendors operating alongside international traders.

The honest challenge with Esquilino is character inconsistency. Some streets are elegant and pleasant; others directly adjacent to Termini have a rougher atmosphere. The solution is granular street-level knowledge when choosing an apartment. Streets around Piazza Vittorio and Via Galilei are significantly better than streets directly bordering Termini. The DVR community network is particularly useful here for this specific research.

Ostiense: Rome's Street Art and Creative Quarter

Ostiense is Rome's answer to Berlin's Neukölln or London's Peckham -- a former industrial zone that has been colonized by creative residents, independent coffee shops, and a thriving arts scene, while remaining affordable enough for the people who actually make the culture. The Outdoor Festival that began here in 2013 has made it Rome's street art capital: over 200 murals by international artists cover entire building facades throughout the neighborhood.

The anchor cultural institution is the Centrale Montemartini (Via Ostiense 106), a former power station now housing classical Roman sculptures in industrial settings -- genuinely one of Rome's best and most undervisited museums. The MACRO Asilo contemporary art space and the Ex-Dogana entertainment venue sit alongside coworking spaces, specialty coffee shops (the density of third-wave coffee is higher here than anywhere in Rome), and a growing cluster of craft businesses.

The expat community in Ostiense skews creative and remote-worker. Several coworking spaces operate within walking distance of most residential addresses. The nightlife scene is excellent without being overwhelming -- music venues, bar clubs, and outdoor spaces that operate Thursday through Sunday without dominating the weekday residential character.

Andreas Mueller, 31, Remote Product Manager (Germany) — Ostiense resident, 14 months

"People tell me they can't imagine living 'that far from the center.' Then I show them that my coworking space is five minutes walk, the Testaccio market is fifteen minutes by bicycle, and I can cycle to Campo de' Fiori in twenty minutes. Distance in Rome is misleading. Ostiense is not far. It is appropriately priced for what it is. What it is happens to be excellent."

San Lorenzo: Rome's University Quarter

San Lorenzo borders La Sapienza, Italy's largest university, which gives the neighborhood its defining character: young, intellectually engaged, cheap, and alive with a nightlife scene that starts at 10pm and ends when it ends. The trattoria culture here is extraordinary -- restaurants feeding students for decades have perfected the art of excellent Roman food at genuinely cheap prices. A three-course lunch with wine costs EUR 12-15 at most of the traditional trattorias.

The expat community in San Lorenzo is the most international of the budget neighborhoods, drawn by university affiliations, creative businesses, and the genuinely affordable rents. The nightlife is a feature for some and a concern for others: the streets around Via dei Volsci and Via degli Equi can be loud on Friday and Saturday nights. Apartments on internal courtyards or above the fourth floor are significantly quieter.

San Lorenzo's architecture is honest working-class Roman: 1930s apartment buildings without the embellishments of Prati or the medieval character of Trastevere. It is not a neighborhood that photographs beautifully. It is a neighborhood where you eat well, pay sensible rent, and live among people who are actually making things rather than performing lifestyle.

Transport from Budget Neighborhoods

Esquilino has the best transport in Rome by a significant margin -- Termini station gives access to Metro A and B, every major bus route, and intercity trains. Journey time to any central Roman attraction is under 20 minutes.

Ostiense is served by the Piramide metro station (Line B, 10-15 minutes walk from most addresses), the Ostiense train station (connections to Fiumicino airport and coastal destinations), and tram lines 3 and 8. Cycling is the preferred transport mode for many Ostiense residents -- the streets are flat and the infrastructure has improved significantly.

San Lorenzo is served by the Tiburtina train and metro station (Line B), extensive bus connections, and a cycling community that has established informal networks of bike lanes and safe routes to the historic center.

The DVR Community in Budget Neighborhoods

The DVR community has a strong and growing presence across all three budget neighborhoods. The Ostiense expat group runs informal coworking mornings twice a week. San Lorenzo hosts a monthly dinner at a rotating trattoria that costs EUR 15-18 per person including wine. Esquilino expats meet informally around the Piazza Vittorio area on weekend mornings.

Budget neighborhood expats are generally more integrated into Roman life than their Trastevere counterparts -- partly by necessity (fewer English-speaking service providers), partly by intent. Many describe the experience of living in a less internationalized neighborhood as accelerating their Italian, deepening their local relationships, and creating a more authentic understanding of Roman life.
