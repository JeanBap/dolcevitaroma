Making Your Apartment Feel Like Home in Rome: Decorating, Furnishing, and Personalizing Your Space

Introduction: From Apartment to Home

The transition from tourism to true expat residency in Rome involves a psychological shift from viewing your apartment as temporary accommodation to understanding it as your home. A blank, unfurnished apartment in Rome, no matter how architecturally beautiful or well-located, requires intentional effort to transform into a comfortable living space reflecting your personality and meeting your practical needs. For expats arriving with minimal possessions or relying on temporary furnished rental arrangements, establishing a home environment becomes essential to long-term psychological wellbeing and integration into Roman life.

Creating a comfortable home in Rome involves navigating Italian and international furnishing options, understanding rental agreement restrictions on decoration and modification, respecting the architectural character of often-historic apartments, and balancing personal style with practical constraints of rental living. This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to know about decorating, furnishing, and personalizing your Roman apartment to create a genuine home rather than simply occupying a rental space.

Understanding Your Rental Agreement: What You Can and Cannot Modify

Before purchasing furniture or undertaking any apartment modifications, review your contratto di locazione (rental contract) regarding decoration and modification permissions. Italian rental agreements typically restrict permanent modifications—painting walls, installing shelving, making structural changes—requiring landlord permission or prohibiting alterations entirely. Some landlords object to any changes; others permit non-permanent modifications that can be reversed upon move-out.

This distinction is crucial. Non-permanent modifications include removable wall decals, temporary shelving with adhesive hooks, movable furniture arrangement, and hangings using picture rails or adhesive strips. Permanent modifications include painting, installing permanent shelving, wallpaper application, and drilling into walls. Thoroughly review your contract and communicate with your landlord about acceptable decoration before purchasing items requiring permanent installation.

Many Roman apartment buildings contain beautiful historic elements—exposed wooden beams, original plaster walls, architectural details developed over centuries. Part of respecting your apartment means preserving and working with these elements rather than covering them with aggressive decoration. The most successful apartment decorating in Rome celebrates existing architectural character while adding personal touches through furniture, soft furnishings, and artwork rather than extensive wall modifications.

Furnishing Your Apartment: Options and Resources

Finding appropriate furniture in Rome involves balancing cost, quality, availability, and delivery logistics. IKEA Rome, located in the EUR neighborhood outside central Rome, is the primary option for affordable, straightforward furniture providing essential items (bed frames, storage, tables, chairs) at reasonable prices. IKEA offers home delivery for large items (approximately 50-100 euros depending on purchase size), removing the challenge of transporting furniture on public transportation or through Rome's narrow streets. IKEA also provides English-language support and simplified purchasing processes familiar to international expats.

However, IKEA is not the only furnishing option. Rome contains numerous Italian furniture stores offering quality pieces with better design often than IKEA's mass-market offerings. These stores include Maisons du Monde (contemporary design with European aesthetic), Calligaris (quality Italian furniture), and various neighborhood furniture shops specializing in specific furniture categories. Prices at Italian furniture stores are typically higher than IKEA but offer superior construction and design.

For expats on tight budgets, secondhand furniture represents excellent option. Facebook expat groups throughout Rome facilitate furniture sales and exchanges within the expat community—outgoing expats selling apartment contents before departure, people upgrading or changing styles offering used furniture at fraction of new prices. Subito.it and Vinted are Italian secondhand resale platforms where Romans sell furniture, clothing, and household goods. Shopping secondhand reduces costs substantially while often providing better quality than new budget options.

Essential Furniture and Practical Furnishing Priorities

Begin furnishing with essential items necessary for daily living: bed, mattress, somewhere to sit (chairs/sofa), table for eating, and storage for belongings. These foundational pieces establish apartment functionality, then additional furnishings can address comfort and aesthetics. Many expats arrive in Rome with only clothing and immediate necessities, purchasing furniture gradually as budget allows and needs become apparent.

Bed selection deserves careful consideration—you'll spend approximately one-third of your time sleeping, making bed quality important for health and wellbeing. Italian mattresses differ from American/British designs, using firmer construction and different materials than many expats expect. Test mattresses in person at furniture stores before purchasing. IKEA's mattress selection provides affordable options including various firmness levels. Higher-end mattress shops offer superior quality but at substantially higher prices.

Storage solutions are consistently challenging in Roman apartments, which often lack closets or provide insufficient storage. Wardrobes (large freestanding closets), shelving units, and chests of drawers address this chronic shortage. Rather than filling apartments with storage, curating your possessions to fit available space helps maintain livable environments. Many long-term Rome expats keep minimal possessions, finding that spacious apartments with few belongings feel more comfortable than cramped spaces overstuffed with furniture.

Creating Comfort Through Soft Furnishings and Textiles

While large furniture establishes functionality, comfort and personality emerge through soft furnishings—bedding, cushions, curtains, rugs, and throws. Quality bedding transforms basic bed frames into comfortable sleep spaces. Thick curtains or blackout shades help regulate summer heat and winter light while providing privacy. Rugs define spaces, add warmth to tile or stone floors common in Roman apartments, and create acoustical improvements in hard-floored spaces that can echo extensively.

Wall hangings—artwork, tapestries, photographs—add character without permanent modification. Pictures can be hung using removable adhesive strips or hooks, satisfying personal expression without damaging walls. Mirrors create light reflection and spatial impression while introducing decorative elements. Plants—both houseplants and flowers on balconies or window sills—add life, color, and psychological wellbeing to apartments while being entirely removable.

Lighting significantly impacts apartment atmosphere. Many Italian apartments contain minimal overhead lighting, relying on a single central ceiling fixture providing harsh, inadequate illumination. Supplementary lighting through table lamps, floor lamps, and desk lamps creates warm, layered lighting enabling comfortable evening activities, reading, and work. String lights and decorative fixtures add ambiance alongside functional illumination.

Decorating on a Budget: Strategic Choices and DIY Solutions

Decorating apartments on limited budgets requires prioritizing visible, frequently-used spaces while accepting unfinished appearance in less visible areas. Bedroom decoration prioritizes the bed itself, surrounding it with comfortable furnishings and wall treatments creating a restful environment. Living room decoration emphasizes seating areas and surfaces where you spend time. Kitchen and bathroom decoration can be minimal if these spaces function adequately, with decoration deferred until budgets allow.

DIY decoration projects reduce costs while enabling personalization. Painting old furniture, creating artwork through printing and framing personal photos, arranging displays of cherished objects, and organizing shelves aesthetically accomplish significant visual impact with minimal cost. Many expats find that simple organization and thoughtful arrangement of possessions creates more appealing spaces than elaborate decoration.

Thrifting and market shopping provide artwork, books, decorative objects, and furnishings at minimal cost. Rome's numerous antique markets, weekend flea markets (Porta Portese, Parco della Musica), and vintage shops offer eclectic options for decoration. Shopping these sources creates unique apartments reflecting your individual taste rather than mass-produced designs.

Addressing Apartment Deficiencies: Problem-Solving and Modifications

Many Roman apartments contain deficiencies—inadequate insulation, poor water pressure, insufficient electrical outlets, limited storage, noise from neighbors or streets. Decorating and furnishing addresses aesthetic and comfort aspects, but practical problems sometimes require problem-solving. Improving water pressure involves cleaning aerators or sometimes negotiating with landlords for upgrades. Limited electrical outlets are addressed through power strips (though excessive use creates fire hazards). Noise is partially addressed through heavy curtains, soft furnishings, and rugs that absorb sound.

Temperature regulation in apartments without air conditioning requires strategic use of fans, window shutters, and cross-ventilation. Bedroom comfort during winter months sometimes requires supplementary space heaters (used safely and carefully) if central heating proves inadequate. These practical problem-solving measures, combined with furnishing and decoration choices, transform marginal apartments into genuinely livable spaces.

Respecting Architectural Character While Adding Personal Style

One of Rome's greatest assets is the architectural character of its buildings and apartments. Historic plaster walls, original wooden doors, tiled floors, and centuries-old details represent irreplaceable elements deserving respect. Successful apartment decoration celebrates these features rather than obscuring them. This might mean choosing minimal wall decoration to showcase exposed plaster, or arranging furniture to emphasize room proportions and architectural details rather than fighting the space's inherent character.

Contemporary design sometimes conflicts with historic apartment character, but thoughtful curation of modern pieces alongside original details creates interesting aesthetic combination. Scandinavian minimalism, industrial design, and eclectic bohemian aesthetics all work well in historic apartments when implemented with respect for existing elements.

Creating Outdoor Spaces: Balconies and Terraces

Many Roman apartments include small balconies or access to shared terraces. These outdoor spaces, however modest, contribute significantly to quality of life. Furnishing outdoor spaces with comfortable seating, potted plants, and attractive arrangements extends living space during warm months. Even tiny balconies accommodate a small chair, plants, and ambiance-creating lighting, creating outdoor retreats from apartment interiors.

Container gardening on balconies allows cultivation of herbs, flowers, and vegetables regardless of garden access. Fresh herbs for cooking, colorful flowers providing visual interest, and the psychological benefits of engaging with plants make balcony gardening worthwhile even for non-expert gardeners. Many Rome neighborhoods host market stalls selling plants, seeds, and gardening supplies at reasonable prices.

Key Takeaways: Home Creation Essentials

  • Review rental contracts regarding acceptable decoration and modifications before purchasing furniture
  • Prioritize essential furniture (bed, seating, storage) before addressing aesthetics
  • Soft furnishings, lighting, and wall decorations create comfort and personality more cost-effectively than large furniture
  • Secondhand furniture from Facebook groups and Italian resale sites provides cost savings and quality
  • Respecting architectural character while adding personal style creates apartments that feel like genuine homes

FAQ: Home Decorating and Furnishing Questions

Is it worth investing in quality furniture for rental apartments? Yes, if you plan multi-year residency. Furniture you'll use for years justifies investment in quality. For shorter-term rentals (under one year), prioritize affordability and functionality. You'll eventually sell or leave furniture anyway, so durability is less critical for temporary situations.

Can I paint my apartment walls if my landlord permits? Yes, if your contract specifically allows it. Use quality paint, maintain original paint samples for restoration upon departure, and understand that you may lose your deposit if walls aren't perfectly restored. Consider whether paint's aesthetic benefit justifies this effort and cost.

What's the best approach for heating apartments without central heating? Layer clothing, invest in quality bedding, use supplementary heaters cautiously (following safety protocols), and use furniture arrangement to create cozy spaces. Space heaters work but increase electricity costs substantially and pose fire risks if not used carefully.

Should I invest in home decor items knowing I might move to another city? Absolutely. Many decor items (artwork, cushions, soft furnishings) are portable and relocate with you. Furniture investment is more problematic if mobility is likely, but items that make your current home pleasant have immediate value regardless of future moves.
